Visual Hierarchy

Amazon

Amazon.com

Visual hierarchy, according to Gestalt psychology, is a pattern in the visual field wherein some elements tend to "stand out," or attract attention, more strongly than other elements, suggesting a hierarchy of importance. Some physical characteristics that highlight and build a hierarchical visual are color, size, alignment and character. Amazon makes call-to-action buttons more prominent by using color

Hick's Law

Lacoste

Lacoste.com

Hick's law describes the time it takes for a person to make a decision as a result of the possible choices: increasing the number of choices will increase the decision time logarithmically. The amount of time taken to process a certain amount of bits in the Hick–Hyman law is known as the rate of gain of information. Hick's law is sometimes cited to justify menu design decisions. Lacoste places a large button with a highlight color to select a menu where the consumer can eliminate distractions and go to what really matters without being distracted.

Fitt's Law

Subway

Subway.com

Fitts's law is a predictive model of human movement primarily used in human–computer interaction and ergonomics. This scientific law predicts that the time required to rapidly move to a target area is a function of the ratio between the distance to the target and the width of the target. Subway places three large buttons, highlighted with bright colors that shorten the distance to the most important of this fast food store
